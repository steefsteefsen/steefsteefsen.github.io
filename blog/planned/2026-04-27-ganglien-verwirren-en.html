Confusing the ganglia

I'm quitting smoking. As of today. The occasional drink goes too. And — this is the point — I'm teaching myself Arch Linux instead.

Not as a reward. As cover.

Addiction doesn't go away. It gets redirected.

There's a story — usually told by therapists, self-help gurus, and wellness apps — that you beat addiction with willpower. You sit it out. Breathe deep. Journal. Feel yourself. Drink water.

That's nonsense. Addiction isn't the craving for nicotine. Addiction is a routine in the brain, burned in over years, that now fires automatically. Stress comes → hand reaches for the pack. Break starts → hand reaches for the pack. Phone call ends → hand reaches for the pack. The pack is the symbol, not the substance. The actual addictive vessel is the gesture, the pause, the small permission to oneself.

Take that away without a replacement and you leave a hole. The hole finds something. If you don't steer it, it grabs sugar. Or doom-scrolling. Or irritability with your family. Or — the classic — the old thing again.

So: Arch.

Arch Linux is the perfect substitute for exactly this mechanism. It has everything a good addiction needs:

Small reward loops. pacman -Syu, a reboot, everything still works — dopamine. yay -S, new tool compiles cleanly — dopamine. Edit a `.conf` file just right and the display manager starts two seconds faster on next boot — dopamine.

Frustration phases with resolution. Three hours of wiki-reading, four forum threads, a Reddit post, then you find it. Exactly the same relief that the cigarette would have triggered after a stretch of stress. Just with a recognisable skill gain at the end.

Self-efficacy as a drug. That's the joke about Arch: you build your system from nothing. Every config line is a decision. After three weeks there is a computer running that nobody else in the world has set up exactly that way. It's the antithesis of the consumption that the old addiction lived on.

No hangover. When I tweak my dwm setup at 1 a.m. and go to bed at 2, I don't feel wrecked the next morning. That is the decisive difference from the alcohol substitute, which I tested and which doesn't work.

Beta test for a platform

What's happening here is — and I'll say this openly — not a self-improvement Sunday stroll. It's an n=1 beta test for a concept I've been carrying around for weeks and that will soon get its own platform:

Addiction reverse-engineering. Instead of denying it — understanding it, mapping its mechanics, deliberately rerouting it onto something that takes you forward.

Working title: diedorismachtdasgift.io (German wordplay on "Doris is making the poison"). Domain not bought yet. Possibly a second domain depending on the audience — because there's a difference between guiding a student off speed and guiding a 50-year-old manager off wine.

Core principle: Nobody quits without starting something else. Addiction is the default. The only question is what for. Anyone who accepts that honestly is already halfway there.

Practical, starting today

I'll write here irregularly over the next weeks about what the substitution does. Not in a "14 days detox" format. More like: what falls away, what comes in, where I notice the redirection working, where it doesn't. Including — honestly — where it fails.

This is not a story about a healed person. It's the real-time documentation of an addict who decided to repurpose his addiction instead of denying it. My suspicion: this scales. If yes, the platform follows.

If not — at least it was a good reason to finally learn Arch. That counts too.
