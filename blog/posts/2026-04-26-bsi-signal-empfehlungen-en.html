When Germany's cyber-security agency recommends Signal

In February 2026, the German Federal Office for Information Security (BSI), jointly with the domestic intelligence service (BfV), published an advisory: recommendations for Signal users targeted by a "likely state-sponsored cyber actor."1 Audience: politicians, military, diplomats, investigative journalists.

Read that again. A federal German agency is explicitly recommending Signal — an open-source messenger with end-to-end encryption designed so that not even the operator can read the messages — to its own at-risk citizens. In writing. On bsi.bund.de.

The punchline

The same German government that recommends Signal as protection has been negotiating Chat Control at the EU level for years — a regulation that would force providers like Signal to scan private messages before encryption.2 Client-side scanning. A backdoor by another name. Signal has stated repeatedly that they would leave the European market rather than break their security model.3

The BSI, meanwhile, writes: change your PIN, enable registration lock, use disappearing messages, hide your phone number. The tools the BSI recommends to at-risk groups are exactly the tools Chat Control would render useless.

One arm of government recommends what the other arm wants to dismantle.

What the advisory actually says

Three scenarios, scaled by damage:

If someone sent you a verification code but you didn't enter it: block and report the contact, enable registration lock or 2FA.1

If you entered the code but still have account access: change your Signal PIN immediately, delete the account (not the app), re-register with a new PIN, consider a new phone number, enable registration lock, hide the phone number on your profile, turn on disappearing messages.1

If you've lost account access: set a new PIN, warn contacts via a different channel, remove deleted accounts from your groups, recreate groups if needed, get a new number and account, request deletion of the old account via Signal Support.1

What this means in practice

The recommendations are technically sound. Anyone connected — even peripherally — to investigative work, activism, whistleblowing, opposition politics, or anything else that displeases someone with money and intelligence-service access should apply them now, not after something happens:

  • Set a Signal PIN, if you haven't. One that isn't recoverable from the password-manager backup of another service.
  • Enable registration lock — prevents anyone from registering a new Signal account on your phone number without your PIN.
  • Hide your phone number on your Signal profile.
  • Disappearing messages as default for sensitive chats.
  • Use a username instead of your phone number for first contact — Signal rolled this out in 2024.4

Why this is also political

The fact that the BSI has to write this advisory at all is an admission: the threat from state-sponsored cyber actors against the country's own population is real, documented, and the protective tool is encryption. The exact encryption that other agencies — through Chat Control, government trojans, mandatory access laws — would like to weaken.

A government that wants to protect encryption for its citizens, rather than break it for its own intelligence operations, writes documents like the BSI advisory. A government that wants to break encryption for its operations writes regulations like Chat Control. You can't have both. There is no backdoor that only the good guys use.5

The BSI is right on this. Follow the advisory. And the next time you read about Chat Control negotiations at an EU interior ministers' meeting, remember where the pressure on Signal comes from — and that the BSI itself does not recommend a single workaround that doesn't rely on strong encryption.
