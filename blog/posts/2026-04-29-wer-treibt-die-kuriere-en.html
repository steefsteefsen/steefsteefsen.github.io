Who's pushing the couriers?

A friend got hit on her bike. She's a courier. She survived. That's all I'm writing about her — she owes you no story.

What I'm writing about is the system that puts her on the bike. And the pace the system demands.

Who's pushing them?

The German delivery industry is shifting away from directly-employed riders toward subcontractors. In 2025, Lieferando announced it would lay off over 2,000 of its own couriers nationwide and switch to a shadow fleet model.1 Couriers report having to pay up to 500 euros for access to an account at these subcontractors, handing over 20 percent of their wages to middlemen, and being paid in cash without a contract. One full-time Lieferando courier — full-time — reports earning 240 to 350 euros a month.2

Pay per item. The algorithm assigning the next ride. The app counting how fast you go. The pace isn't accidentally high. The pace is the business model.

What the pace costs

Germany's Berufsgenossenschaft Verkehr (the statutory accident insurer for the transport sector) ran a special analysis between July 2022 and June 2023. The result: bicycles and pedelecs are involved in more on-the-job traffic accidents than heavy trucks. 685 recorded insurance cases, scaled to roughly 7,650 accidents in a single year.3

That's the bookkeeping of pace. It's not about individual error. It's about an industry that consumes bodies faster than they can recover.

What's moving

The EU Platform Work Directive came into force in 2024. Member states have to transpose it by the end of 2026. It targets bogus self-employment and gives platform workers a legal presumption of employee status.4 Berlin is examining a Bundesrat initiative for a direct-employment requirement in delivery work.1 NGG and ver.di are organising at the subcontractors that carry the model.5

All of that is good. It's happening. It's not enough.

What you can do

What you personally can do is not: order faster, tip bigger, or "only order from the good ones." What you can do is: lower the pressure in your own life, so you can notice the people around you.

The person bringing your food is not alone in the world. Your friend who's a courier is not alone. The neighbours you don't greet are not alone. They have stories. Injuries. If you forget that, you also forget that you yourself are not alone.

Stop scrolling. Bring yourself to a tempo where you can see people. Listen to slow music. Concretely: this set, mori — something slow 0178,6 is the pace this piece is written at. Read on while it plays. It makes room for the arguments.

My offer

If something serious lands — for you or for someone close to you — a conflict, an accident, a break that can't be carried alone:

I'll cook and make tea. You bring the people involved in the conflict, or their chosen representatives. There is strength in stillness.

That's not a programme. That's a kitchen. But a kitchen is more than nothing. A kitchen is the place where the pace comes down.

Who's pushing the couriers — that's not you. But who slows down — that's you.
