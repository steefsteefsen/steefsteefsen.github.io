Version 1.0 — and two algorithms I trust

Earlier today the Journeys page shipped. Three trips — the U.S. with my father, the Caribbean alone, Brussels via the Seychelles to South Africa — plus a timeline that puts career and journeys on a single axis. Version 1.0, no editing pass.

I think about editing in three stages.

Stage 1 — myself. In the browser, with quiet, a few read-throughs. Mandatory, but it sits too close to the text to catch its own mistakes.

Stage 2 — the people the text is about. Whoever appears in the text should read it before anyone else does. I'm catching that up. Until then the Journeys page says, in two places: "I leave her name out here until she releases it." Not a face-saving trick — it's the new standard on this site.

Stage 3 — the swarm. Publication itself. What comes back shapes 1.1. Without stage 3, every page stays a monologue.

1.0 just passed stage 1, has stage 2 for the uncritical parts, and starts stage 3 by going public. If you find a mistake, write me.

Why these two algorithms, and not the others

I trust my SoundCloud and my YouTube music algorithm more than the feed LinkedIn or Google curate for me.

The difference is not purity — both sides monetise my listening, both have commercial interests, neither is my friend. The difference is what they optimise for. Music recommendations optimise for a very simple question: do you want to hear that again? If the answer is yes, the algorithm is satisfied. Career and search feeds optimise for something else: what makes you scroll on, click on, react? That isn't the same as what helps you.

Consequence: when I build a piece of software that's meant to carry warmth — a journeys page, an idols page, a blog post — I let sound from the first kind of system run, not the second. Whatever plays in the background prints on the work.

What was happening outside while it was being built

Three notes from the edges — not as a news roundup, just as context.

ZenBusiness, hacked. Have I Been Pwned has documented the ZenBusiness breach: in March 2026 the extortion group ShinyHunters exfiltrated data from the US business-formation platform; when the ransom went unpaid, they released it publicly in April. Roughly five million email addresses, often with names and phone numbers. Pertinent for me as a data engineer: the hackers' supply chain runs through Snowflake, Mixpanel and Salesforce — exactly the platforms we work with daily at Siemens Energy. Anyone who registered a US LLC via ZenBusiness should check their email at HIBP.

The Academy draws a line. This week: a reflection piece by Business Punk on LinkedIn — "Why the Academy's AI ban is the saving of the creative" (in German). The gist: Hollywood is excluding AI-generated work from the creative categories. I haven't verified the AMPAS resolution text directly — but if it lands the way the piece reads it, it's a striking counter-pole to what one perceives as the general "AI wave". The industry whose business is storytelling defends a line that other industries have long since given up.

5,000 fewer US soldiers in Germany. Reuters reported on 1 May 2026 that the US under Trump is withdrawing about 5,000 soldiers from Germany. I read the article; the link is behind Reuters' paywall — anyone who wants to read it openly will have to search elsewhere. What it means when the American footprint in Germany shrinks is not a question for a May morning. It's a question that will work for five, ten years.

Three very different things — a data breach, a creative boundary, a military withdrawal. What links them: in each, someone else is deciding about something I'm part of. Data I entered. An industry I don't work in but whose tools I know. A country I live in. Maybe that's also why, when I build, I'd rather listen to music than to the news — music isn't about me, but it makes room.

What happens next

The Journeys page will find its place on the homepage once I've built the architecture for it — What I stand for, then How I got there (= journeys), then CV. Until then it lives at /journeys/, directly reachable but not yet in the footer. A stretch of in-between.

Whoever listens, listens to two sources at once. Whoever wants to build, is invited.
